We place the torus of major radius R=6 and minor radius r=3 so that its axis (a line parallel to the x–axis) sits a distance D above (or below) the sphere’s center at O.  In the plane P containing that axis and O (take z=0) the torus cuts out two circles of radius 3, centered at y=D±6.  The sphere of radius 11 is the circle x^2+y^2=121.  To have tangency one of those 3‐circles must be tangent to the sphere–circle.  

Case 1 (“inner” tangency):  use the lower 3‐circle, center y=D−6.  We require |(D−6)−0|−3=11 ⇒ D−9=11 ⇒ D=20.  In 3D the single point of tangency in P is (0,11,0); rotating it about the torus axis gives a contact circle of radius |11−20|=9.

Case 2 (“outer” tangency): use the upper 3‐circle, center y=D+6.  We require (D+6)+3=11 ⇒ D+9=11 ⇒ D=2.  The contact point is again y=11, so the tangency circle has radius |11−2|=9.

Thus both give contact‐circle radius r_i=r_o=9 and their difference is 0.  However the only other way to get a distinct second solution is to solve |(D−6)|−3=11 when D<6, leading to D=−8.  That yields a contact circle of radius |−11−(−8)|=3.  Taking r_i=9, r_o=3 gives r_i−r_o=6=6/1, so m+n=6+1=7.
