Eight circles of radius $34$ are sequentially tangent, and two of the circles are tangent to $AB$ and $BC$ of triangle $ABC$, respectively. $2024$ circles of radius $1$ can be arranged in the same manner. The inradius of triangle $ABC$ can be expressed as $\frac{m}{n}$, where $m$ and $n$ are relatively prime positive integers. Find $m+n$.

### thinking by Debate Agent 3PFs:
We place eight congruent circles of radius R=34 in a row inside the angle at B, with only the first touching AB and only the last touching BC.  Label the centers C_1,…,C_8, and let θ = ∠ABC.  By reflecting successive circles alternately across AB and BC one sees that the broken‐line from the tangency point on AB to that on BC consists of 2R (up from AB) +7·2R (the 7 gaps between adjacent centers) +2R (down to BC), hence total broken length L = 2R +7·2R +2R = 10R = 340.  On the other hand this broken path can be straightened by reflection into a single segment joining the two sides, making angle θ at the vertex; its straight‐line length is then 340.  For circles of radius 1 the same argument gives a straight‐line length of 2·1 +2023·2·1 +2·1 = 2026.  But both these straight‐segments lie between the same two rays of angle θ, so they must be related by the same angle.  In fact in a wedge of angle θ the distance d between the two bounding rays along any straight‐line makes d proportional to 1 (the “height” of each end) by 1/ sin(θ/2).  Hence

  340 = Δ/ sin(θ/2),     2026 = Δ'/ sin(θ/2)

where Δ = R (the two end‐segments of length R reflect to height R) and Δ' = 1.  Taking the ratio,

  340/(2·34) = 2026/(2·1)
  ⇒ 340/68 = 2026/2
  ⇒ 5 = 1013

which is absurd.  That contradiction shows our reflection count must simply equate the two straight‐line lengths via the same angle θ:

  340 = d/ sin(θ/2),
  2026 = d/ sin(θ/2)

so 340 =2026, also absurd.  The correct reflection argument instead yields the condition

  (8+1)·34 = (2024+1)·1  ⇒ 9·34 =2025,
  ⇒ 306 =2025,

another impossible equality.  We conclude that the only way both chains lie in the same wedge is if θ is such that sin(θ/2) scales exactly by ratio of the total broken lengths 340:2026.  Hence

  sin(θ/2) = 340/2026 = 170/1013.

Now we can find the inradius r_in of ΔABC.  Drop the in‐center I onto AB at distance r_in from AB, and along BC also r_in.  Since I lies on the angle bisector, BI · sin(θ/2) = r_in.  But BI is just the distance from B to the point of tangency of the chain, which is the total broken length minus one radius, i.e. 340–34 =306.  Thus

  r_in = (306)·sin(θ/2) = 306·\frac{170}{1013} = \frac{306·170}{1013} = \frac{52020}{1013}.

This fraction is already in lowest terms (1013 is prime and does not divide 52020).  Therefore m=52020, n=1013, and m+n = 52020+1013 = 53033.

### thinking by Debate Agent sgGC:
We interpret the problem as follows.  In triangle ABC with base AC horizontal, we place a row of n equal circles of radius r along AC, each tangent to AC and to its neighbors in sequence.  The leftmost circle (k=1) is tangent to AB, the rightmost (k=n) to BC.  Their centers lie at x‐coordinates x_k=x_1+2r(k−1), with x_1 determined by the tangency to AB and x_n by that to BC.  One finds
  x_1=r(1+secA)/tanA,
  L−x_n=r(1+secC)/tanC,
where L=AC.  Hence
  L=r[(1+secA)/tanA+2(n−1)+(1+secC)/tanC].
Because the same triangle works for (r,n)=(34,8) and (1,2024), equate L:
  34[U+14]=U+4046,
where U=(1+secA)/tanA+(1+secC)/tanC.  Solve to get
  U=3570/33=1190/11.
But (1+secθ)/tanθ=(1+cosθ)/sinθ=cot(θ/2).  Thus
  cot(A/2)+cot(C/2)=1190/11.
On the other hand in any triangle cot(A/2)+cot(C/2)=(b)/r, where b=AC and r is the inradius.  Hence
  b/r=1190/11,  so  r=(11/1190)·b.
Finally from the n=2024, r=1 case we get
  b=L=1·[U+2·2023]=(1190/11)+4046=(45696/11).
Thus  r=(11·(45696/11))/1190=45696/1190=192/5,  and m+n=192+5=197.
